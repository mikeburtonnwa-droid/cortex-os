# Voice Guide

The writing-style skill governs sentence-level mechanics. The voice-guide skill governs the deliverable's tone, register, and persona for its specific audience.

## Audience awareness

Every deliverable has an audience. Name it before drafting.

Internal teammates: register can be casual, direct, abbreviated. Domain shorthand is fine.

Internal leadership: register is direct but formal. No shorthand. Lead with the recommendation or the ask.

External clients: register is formal but not stuffy. Lead with the answer. Frame for the client's role and interests. Cite sources.

External investors: register is precise and quantified. Every claim is sourced. Confidence is explicit. Hedges only with numbers or conditionals.

Public audience: register matches the venue. A LinkedIn post is conversational. A whitepaper is formal. A pitch deck is visual-heavy with terse text.

## Confidence levels

External-facing analysis ships with explicit confidence on every finding. Three levels: high, medium, low.

High: multiple independent sources confirm, primary data is recent, methodology is defensible. Use when the finding can survive a hostile peer review.

Medium: sources confirm but with caveats. Some data is stale, methodology has known weaknesses, or only one or two independent sources exist. Use when the finding is the best available answer but a reasonable analyst could disagree.

Low: limited evidence, significant gaps, or strong reasons to doubt the available sources. Use when the operator should treat the finding as a working hypothesis, not a conclusion.

A finding without a confidence label is an opinion. Opinions are removed from external deliverables.

## Hedge language

Hedges do work or they do not. Remove hedges that do no work; keep hedges that ground the claim.

Hedge with no work: "The market may shift in the coming months."

Hedge with work: "If the proposed tariff passes in Q3, the market structure reverses within two quarters."

The first sentence says nothing. The second is a falsifiable claim.

## Persona consistency

A deliverable has a persona implicit in its voice. The persona is consistent across the document. A memo that opens formally and drifts conversational reads as drafted in two minds.

The four reference personas in cortex-os:

The orchestrator persona: structured, dispatch-oriented, neutral.

The analyst persona: skeptical, evidence-bound, quantified.

The researcher persona: precise, citation-disciplined, source-aware.

The writer persona: clear, direct, audience-aware.

The reviewer persona: adversarial, blunt, severity-tiered.

When drafting under a persona, hold it throughout. Style drift across sections is a sign of multiple drafters; consolidate before delivery.

## Register markers

The register of a deliverable is set by a small number of word choices.

Casual register: contractions, second person, sentence fragments are fine.

Formal register: contractions sparingly, third person, complete sentences.

Technical register: passive voice for procedures, active voice for findings, named entities and quantified claims throughout.

Match the register to the audience and hold it.

## When the voice bends

Voice bends for two reasons.

First: the audience changes within a single deliverable. A client report's executive summary is formal; the appendix can be more technical. Mark the transitions explicitly.

Second: the operator overrides for tactical reasons. A normally-formal voice goes blunt for one paragraph to emphasize stakes. Use sparingly; the contrast is the lever.
